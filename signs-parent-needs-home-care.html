7 Signs Your Aging Parent May Need Help at Home

Domira Home Care · March 2026 · North Texas

It usually doesn't happen overnight. You visit your parents and notice things are different. The house isn't as tidy. They seem slower, more forgetful. Here are seven signs it might be time to consider in-home care.

1. Difficulty with Personal Hygiene

Skipping showers, wearing the same clothes for days, unkempt appearance, or noticeable body odor. When someone who always took pride in their appearance starts letting personal care slide, it's often because the physical tasks have become too difficult or they're forgetting.

2. Changes in Eating Habits

Unexplained weight loss, expired food in the fridge, missed meals, or difficulty cooking. Nutrition is fundamental to health, and when eating habits decline, everything else follows.

3. Increased Forgetfulness

Missing medications, forgetting appointments, leaving the stove on, or repeating the same conversations. Some forgetfulness is normal with aging, but when it starts affecting safety and daily function, it's a sign that support is needed.

4. Declining Mobility

Unsteadiness on their feet, holding onto furniture to walk, falls or near-falls, difficulty getting up from chairs. Mobility issues are one of the biggest risk factors for serious injury in older adults. A single fall can be life-changing.

5. Social Withdrawal

Avoiding activities they used to enjoy, not answering the phone, losing interest in hobbies, refusing to leave the house. Social isolation is linked to depression, cognitive decline, and increased health risks.

6. Messy or Unsafe Home

Piled up mail, dirty dishes, cluttered walkways, burned pots, missed household maintenance. A declining home environment is often one of the first visible signs that someone needs help.

7. Caregiver Burnout

If you're the primary caregiver and you're feeling exhausted, overwhelmed, or unable to keep up with your own life, that's a sign too. You can't pour from an empty cup. Getting professional support isn't giving up. It's making sure your loved one gets consistent, quality care while you take care of yourself.
